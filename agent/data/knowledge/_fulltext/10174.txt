Summary Sheet
The growth of personal devices, from handheld mobile phones to electric vehicles, has
led to an increasing demand for “free” electricity from public charging stations. In response,
M
public places like airports and coffee shops are increasing the availability of electrical outlets for
their visitors. However, this “free” energy is actually paid for by private businesses and the
government. Consequently, it is important to study the rapid growth of eClectricity energy
consumption and to understand its effects on national level, as well as investigate approaches to
M
mitigate the cost. Our objective is to develop a mathematical model to predict the economic costs
of increasing public electrical outlet availability over the next decade and investigate ways to
reduce the impact and its cost. i
H
Our model consists of a continuous time-dependent function that incorporates 1) a
computational agent-based model to characterize the small-scale interactions between people and
：
device charging availability in a single public location and 2) an analytical model describing
electric vehicle charging. For the agent-based model, we explore several location-specific
parameters that vary across different publi号c locations and analyze the differences in cost across
locations and with increasing outlet availability. We integrate our models to predict total
economic cost due to public charging of personal devices and provide suggested guidelines to
reduce cost. Our model accounts f众or many parameters such as the influx distribution of visitors
to a specific destination that is backed up by extensive research and is thus highly robust and
applicable to model different situations.
公
Overall, our model predicts that energy consumption due to the charging of personal
electronic devices and electric vehicles will increase in the coming years. For cost due to electric
charging of personal devices, we found that schools and airports suffer the most costs of
信
increasing demand and that public outlet density was strongly correlated to an increase in
electrical consumption. For low public outlet densities, we found an electricity cost for personal
devices to 微grow from $150,000 to $180,000 over a ten year period. On the other hand, for high
outlet density, we determined a price change from $450,000 to $525,000 per year. For electric
vehicles, we estimated a cost change from $50,000,000 to $400,000,000 per year between 2018
and 2028. We also determined that the ideal outlet density for airports and coffee was 3 outlets
per 576 ft2 and 3 outlets per 144 ft2 respectively. For electric vehicles our model was able to
predict the total energy consumption of 2018 within 0.048 TWh. We also found that the overall
cost associated with publicly charging electronic devices was relatively low due to the low price
of generating electricity. While the associated cost of publicly funded charging is relatively
small, it can be further decreased through restrictions on public outlets by public area
management and the integration of increasingly efficient technologies in the coming years.

Personal electronic devices, from the phones and laptops we see and use everyday to the
slightly less ubiquitous electric cars, have become increasingly prevalent in the last few years. In
accordance with this increase in device presence, public areas such as airports and coffee shops
have begun to increase electrical charging outlets to satisfy the resulting demand. Unfortunately,
this “free” energy is actually paid for by business and governments.
We were tasked with answering the question of, “How does the cost of offering free
electricity change over time?” To do so, we first wanted to understand the reasons behind this
change in electricity cost over time. We looked into the change in the price of electricity over
time and change in the attendance of a location over time. Next, we identified different public
spaces and characterized how each location differed from the others in terms of electricity usage.
M
We settled on airports, libraries, schools, railway stations and coffee shops as the public
destinations we wanted to model.
C
To build our model, we wanted to understand the different factors that influence electrical
energy consumption: number of visitors and distribution throughout a day, number of outlets, the
M
number of personal electronic devices and corresponding energy needed. With these ideas in
mind, we created a Netlogo model to calculate the usage of electricity while incorporating the
i
complexity of each variable. In this agent based model, we were able to simulate the actions of
H
individuals with a set number of devices with random charge distributions and their appearance
in each of the destinations. While this model could： incorporate the factors of energy
consumption for individuals in public spaces, it could not be used to model electric car energy
consumption.
We created a second model to mod号el the consumption of electricity to charge electric cars
30%, a safe estimate to travel from the charging station back home. Using the time it takes to
charge a car battery and the average number of available charging stations, we estimated the
众
amount of energy used to charge electric cars in public during one day across the nation. With
this data, we calculated the average electric consumption for the United States in one year.
With our models, we estimated the cost of electricity for electric vehicles to be
公
$50,000,00 in 2018 and for this value to increase to 400,000,000 in 2028. We also found
electricity cost for personal devices to grow from $150,000 to $180,000 over the same ten year
period. Using othe信r calculations and data from our model, we found that outlet density strongly
correlated to increase in costs of electricity and that Schools and Airports consisted of the
majority of public charging costs due to their large volumes.
微
With our model in hand, we advise two main methods to reduce costs associated with
charging electronic devices. Primarily, business and other public places can restrict access to
electrical outlets thereby reducing their overhead with visitors utilizing their electricity.
Additionally, we predict that electronics efficiency will increase in the coming years with new
advances in batteries and other technologies which will reduce the cost associated with charging
electronic devices.
